# Product

## Register

brand

## Users

Potential clients evaluating a freelance designer/developer for real work: business owners who need a brand identity or website, bars and restaurants looking for custom signage, art directors with a specific project, and direct-to-client companies who want a specialist rather than an agency. They arrive with a job to get done, not a design education to receive. They're assessing fit, trust, and craft quality in under 90 seconds.

Secondary audience: peer designers and developers who may collaborate or refer work.

## Product Purpose

paulyddesign.com is Paul Danowski's portfolio and lead-generation site. It exists to convert qualified visitors into clients. Success means a potential client reads enough to feel confident, sees work that resonates, and reaches out. The site is the first piece of work a client sees — it has to perform as well as the work it contains.

Services span digital (brand identity, logo design, web design & development, 3D rendering, illustration) and physical craft (reverse gilded glass signs, gold leaf lettering). The physical/craft angle is a genuine differentiator; most digital designers can't do it. It earns mention and presence without dominating.

## Brand Personality

Bold · Honest · Obsessed

Strong opinions, direct voice, evident love of the medium. No filler, no apologies. The work leads — headlines don't oversell it. The personality shows in specificity (15+ years, Philadelphia 76ers, Villanova Athletics, hand-applied gold leaf) rather than in adjectives. A designer who knows what they're doing doesn't need to announce it.

## Anti-references

- Generic agency sites — hero copy that reads "We craft experiences." Stock motion. Amorphous 3D blobs.
- Behance/Dribbble showcase mode — pixel-perfect hero shots with no context, no client, no story; style over substance.
- Overcrowded freelancer sites — every skill listed, every service screamed; no restraint, no hierarchy, no reason to trust the design taste.
- Trendy / ephemeral aesthetics — glassmorphism, bento grids, brutalist overload, anything that dates in 18 months.

## Design Principles

1. **The work is the argument.** Let images and specifics persuade; skip the adjectives. If a project image is absent, the copy around it must earn its place with concrete detail, not marketing language.
2. **Restraint is confidence.** One strong element per section. Not every idea needs to be expressed. Negative space is not emptiness — it's editorial discipline.
3. **Craft shows at every scale.** The same care that goes into a gilded sign goes into a hover state. No lazy defaults, no unexamined spacing, no "good enough" anything.
4. **Physical and digital, one voice.** The site doesn't treat the glass signs as a quirky sidebar. They share the same visual weight as the digital work. The unusual range is the point.
5. **Direct, not cute.** Copy is honest and specific. The site talks to someone who has a real project, not someone browsing for inspiration. CTA language should sound like how Paul actually talks.

## Accessibility & Inclusion

WCAG 2.1 AA as baseline. Dark background with gold accents requires careful contrast checking — bone text (#F0EDE6) on dark-bg (#0E0E0C) passes easily; muted text variants (rgba(240,237,230,0.45) and lower) must be validated against their actual backgrounds. Reduced-motion support required for all entrance animations. Keyboard navigability for all interactive elements.
